# Debt Rules in a Small Open Economy

## Question and promised objects

The paper claims that a tighter interest-rate rule stabilizes public debt at a modest output cost. It promises transition paths for debt, output, consumption, and household welfare.

## Environment

Government debt at the start of period t is B_t. The government budget displayed in the paper is B_{t+1}=B_t+G_t-T_t. The text states that debt carries interest at rate r, but interest payments do not appear elsewhere in the government budget or resource constraint.

## Policy experiment

The policy shock is a one-unit increase in the interest-rate rule. Figures label the impulse as both one percentage point and 100 basis points, while the code description says the rule variable is stored in decimals and receives a value of 1. We report a peak output response of -0.8 percent. The same rule also enters the household borrowing rate directly, but the paper attributes the entire output response to improved expectations about government debt and does not separate the direct borrowing-cost channel.

## Transition

The perfect-foresight transition is computed for 30 periods with terminal debt fixed at its original steady-state value. The experiment permanently raises government purchases, but no terminal adjustment or tax path is described. The paper states that every point on the reported transition satisfies all equilibrium conditions.

## Results and scope

The output impulse response is negative for the first four periods and positive thereafter; its reported cumulative response is positive. The text nevertheless says that output falls at every horizon and that both the peak and cumulative results establish a small contraction. Consumption and welfare paths promised at the outset are not reported. The conclusion applies the claimed debt-stabilization mechanism and output magnitude to small open economies generally, although the analysis uses one calibration and no cross-economy validation.
