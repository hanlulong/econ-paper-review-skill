# Reminders and Worker Saving

## Abstract

We randomly assigned 240 volunteers at one factory to receive a weekly saving reminder or no reminder. The reminder increased reported saving after one week by 9 percentage points. We conclude that reminders increase saving for all manufacturing workers.

## Design and measurement

Assignment was individual and generated independently. Saving was self-reported in a follow-up survey. Immediately before the saving questions, treated respondents alone were asked which feature of the reminder helped them save; control respondents proceeded directly to the outcomes. The survey collected both an indicator for any saving and the dollar amount saved, but the paper reports only the indicator and gives no reason for omitting the amount. We removed respondents in the top and bottom 5 percent of reported dollar saving within each assignment arm, then estimated the any-saving effect in that trimmed sample. Of 120 assigned participants per arm, 103 treated and 78 control participants completed follow-up. We analyze respondents and do not adjust for nonresponse because treatment was randomized.

## Results

The difference in the any-saving rate among retained respondents is 9 percentage points. Conventional heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors give a p-value of 0.04. The any-saving rate pooled across the two arms is 82 percent; arm-specific means are not reported, and the paper describes the 9-point effect as an 11 percent increase by dividing it by that pooled rate. The result is robust to a Huber regression run on the same trimmed respondent sample.

## Conclusion

The experiment shows that low-cost reminders raise saving for workers generally.
